First Intifada

PROGRESSION OF THE WAR

The First Intifada (1987-1993) was a Palestinian uprising against Israeli occupation in the West Bank and Gaza Strip. It began spontaneously following an incident in which an Israeli military vehicle killed four Palestinian workers, sparking widespread protests and acts of civil disobedience. The movement was characterized by mass demonstrations, strikes, and boycotts, as well as violent confrontations with Israeli forces.

Israel responded with harsh measures, including curfews, arrests, and military crackdowns. Meanwhile, the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) gained international recognition during this period as it sought to negotiate a resolution to the conflict. The uprising also saw the emergence of Hamas as a major political and militant force, challenging the PLO’s dominance in Palestinian politics.

The Intifada ended with the signing of the Oslo Accords in 1993, which established the framework for limited Palestinian self-rule in parts of the West Bank and Gaza. While the accords marked a diplomatic breakthrough, they left many core issues unresolved, setting the stage for future conflicts.
