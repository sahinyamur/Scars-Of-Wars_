Iraq War

PROGRESSION OF THE WAR

The Iraq War, which began in March 2003, was launched by the United States and its allies under the pretext of eliminating weapons of mass destruction (WMDs) and toppling Saddam Hussein’s regime. Despite widespread international opposition and insufficient evidence of WMDs, the U.S. proceeded with the invasion, emphasizing the need to prevent Iraq from posing a threat to global security. The initial military campaign, known as "shock and awe," quickly overwhelmed Iraqi forces, leading to the fall of Baghdad within weeks.

While the invasion succeeded in removing Saddam from power, it plunged Iraq into chaos. Sectarian tensions between Sunni and Shia communities, long suppressed under Saddam’s rule, erupted into violent conflict. Insurgent groups targeted coalition forces and civilians, making stabilization efforts exceedingly difficult. The U.S. attempted to establish a democratic government and rebuild Iraq’s infrastructure, but corruption and insecurity hindered progress. The war also strained U.S. relations with many of its allies and sparked global protests.

By the time U.S. troops withdrew in 2011, Iraq remained unstable, with frequent sectarian violence and the rise of extremist groups like ISIS. The war had a profound impact on the region, destabilizing neighboring countries and reshaping the global discourse on military intervention.
