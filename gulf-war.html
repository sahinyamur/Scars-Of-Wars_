Gulf War

PROGRESSION OF THE WAR

The Gulf War, which began in August 1990, was triggered by Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait. Led by Saddam Hussein, Iraq justified its actions by accusing Kuwait of overproducing oil and stealing Iraqi oil reserves through slant drilling. The invasion shocked the international community, as Iraq's aggression posed a direct threat to regional stability and global oil supplies. The United Nations condemned the invasion and imposed economic sanctions on Iraq, demanding its immediate withdrawal. When diplomatic efforts failed, a U.S.-led coalition of 39 countries launched a military operation to liberate Kuwait.

The war began with Operation Desert Storm in January 1991, a massive air campaign targeting Iraqi forces and infrastructure. The coalition utilized advanced military technology, ensuring air superiority and minimizing their own casualties. This was followed by a swift ground offensive that forced Iraqi troops to retreat from Kuwait in just 100 hours. Despite Iraq's attempts to slow the coalition's advance by setting Kuwaiti oil fields ablaze, the coalition achieved a decisive victory.

The Gulf War ended in February 1991 with Kuwait’s liberation, but the conflict had lasting consequences. While Saddam Hussein's regime was weakened, he remained in power, and Iraq faced crippling economic sanctions. The war also underscored the complexities of Middle Eastern geopolitics, as regional tensions persisted, setting the stage for future conflicts in the Gulf region.
