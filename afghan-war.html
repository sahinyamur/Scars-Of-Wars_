Afghan War

PROGRESSION OF THE WAR

The Afghan War was initiated by the United States and its allies in October 2001 in response to the September 11 terrorist attacks. The primary goal was to dismantle Al-Qaeda and remove the Taliban regime, which had provided a safe haven for the terrorist group. The invasion began with airstrikes and special forces operations that quickly toppled the Taliban government. Kabul fell within weeks, and a new interim government was established under Hamid Karzai.

However, the war soon transitioned into a prolonged insurgency as the Taliban regrouped and launched guerrilla attacks across the country. The conflict was marked by a deadly cycle of violence, civilian casualties, and corruption within the Afghan government. Efforts to rebuild Afghanistan’s infrastructure and institutions were undermined by widespread insecurity and the failure to address local grievances. Over the years, NATO forces struggled to maintain control, even as billions of dollars were invested in training Afghan security forces.

In 2021, after nearly two decades of fighting, U.S. and NATO troops withdrew from Afghanistan, leading to the swift collapse of the Afghan government and the Taliban’s return to power. The war, which cost countless lives and displaced millions, left Afghanistan in political and economic disarray. For many, it symbolized the limits of foreign intervention and the challenges of nation-building in deeply divided societies.
