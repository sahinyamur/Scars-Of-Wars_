Second Intifada

PROGRESSION OF THE WAR

The Second Intifada (2000-2005) erupted after Israeli opposition leader Ariel Sharon’s visit to the Temple Mount, a site sacred to both Jews and Muslims. Palestinians saw the visit as a provocation, leading to widespread protests and a surge in violence. Unlike the First Intifada, the Second Intifada was far bloodier, with frequent suicide bombings, armed clashes, and Israeli military operations.

Israel intensified its security measures, including building a separation barrier in the West Bank and launching targeted assassinations against Palestinian leaders. Meanwhile, militant groups such as Hamas and Islamic Jihad carried out high-profile attacks, including bus bombings and rocket launches. Civilians on both sides bore the brunt of the conflict, with thousands killed and injured.

The violence eventually subsided in 2005 after international mediation and unilateral moves by Israel, including the withdrawal from Gaza. However, the Second Intifada left deep scars, further entrenching divisions and mistrust between Israelis and Palestinians.
